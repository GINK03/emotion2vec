You should be able to quickly determine how\neach metric or event was captured and what it represents. During an\noutage you won\u2019t want to spend time figuring out what your\ndata means. Keep your metrics and events as simple as possible, use\nstandard concepts described above, and name them clearly.\n Granular. If you collect metrics too infrequently or average\nvalues over long windows of time, you may lose important information\nabout system behavior. For example, periods of 100% resource\nutilization will be obscured if they are averaged with periods of\nlower utilization. Collect metrics for each system at a frequency\nthat will not conceal problems, without collecting so often that\nmonitoring becomes perceptibly taxing on the system (the observer\neffect)\nor creates noise in your monitoring data by sampling time intervals\nthat are too short to contain meaningful data.\n Tagged by scope. Each of your hosts operates simultaneously in\nmultiple scopes, and you may want to check on the aggregate health\nof any of these scopes, or their combinations. For example: how is\nproduction doing in aggregate? How about production in the Northeast\nU.S.? How about a particular software/hardware combination? It is\nimportant to retain the multiple scopes associated with your data so\nthat you can alert on problems from any scope, and quickly\ninvestigate outages without being limited by a fixed hierarchy\nof hosts.\n Long-lived. Keep your metrics and events as simple as possible, use\n>    standard concepts described above, and name them clearly.\n>-   **Granular.** If you collect metrics too infrequently or average\n>    values over long windows of time, you may lose important information\n>    about system behavior. For example, periods of 100% resource\n>    utilization will be obscured if they are averaged with periods of\n>    lower utilization. Collect metrics for each system at a frequency\n>    that will not conceal problems, without collecting so often that\n>    monitoring becomes perceptibly taxing on the system (the [observer\n>    effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observer_effect_(information_technology)))\n>    or creates noise in your monitoring data by sampling time intervals\n>    that are too short to contain meaningful data.\n>-   **Tagged by scope.** Each of your hosts operates simultaneously in\n>    multiple scopes, and you may want to check on the aggregate health\n>    of any of these scopes, or their combinations. For example: how is\n>    production doing in aggregate? How about production in the Northeast\n>    U.S.? How about a particular software/hardware combination? It is\n>    important to retain the multiple scopes associated with your data so\n>    that you can alert on problems from any scope, and quickly\n>    investigate outages without being limited by a fixed hierarchy\n>    of hosts.\n>-   **Long-lived.** If you discard data too soon, or if after a period\n>    of time your monitoring system aggregates your metrics to reduce\n>    storage costs, then you lose important information about what\n>    happened in the past. Retaining your raw data for a year or more\n>    makes it much easier to know what \u201cnormal\u201d is, especially if your\n>    metrics have monthly, seasonal, or annual variations.\n\n-
